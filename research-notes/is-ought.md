# Motivation and the Is–Ought Distinction

## Research Question

Can a philosophical theory of motivation remain entirely descriptive without making normative claims?

## Central Problem

Many theories of human motivation begin by explaining why people behave as they do but gradually transition into claims about how people ought to behave.

This transition is philosophically significant.

To explain why an individual acts from self-preservation, duty, or symbolic meaning is one thing. To conclude that one of these motivations ought to prevail is another.

The Interpretive Motivational Systems Framework (IMSF) aims to avoid this transition by remaining descriptive throughout its explanatory scope.

Its purpose is not to determine which motivations are morally superior, but to explain how competing motivations are organized into a single behavioural outcome.

---

## The Is–Ought Problem

David Hume famously argued that statements describing what **is** cannot, by themselves, justify conclusions about what **ought** to be.

Motivational theories sometimes risk crossing this boundary.

For example:

> "Humans are motivated by self-preservation; therefore they ought to pursue self-preservation."

Or:

> "Meaning is humanity's highest motivation; therefore meaning ought to guide behaviour."

Neither conclusion follows logically from the descriptive premise alone.

The explanation of motivation and the justification of moral obligation remain distinct philosophical projects.

---

## The Position of the IMSF

The IMSF is proposed as a descriptive framework.

Its explanatory objective is limited to understanding:

- how multiple motivational systems become active,
- how they compete,
- how secondary modifiers influence that competition,
- and how behaviour ultimately emerges.

The framework deliberately avoids prescribing which motivational system should prevail.

Whether survival, duty, or meaning ought to be prioritised depends upon ethical theories lying outside the scope of the framework itself.

---

## Why This Distinction Matters

Maintaining a descriptive approach offers several advantages.

It allows the framework to remain compatible with diverse ethical traditions rather than committing itself to any particular moral doctrine.

The same explanatory architecture could therefore be applied to individuals with radically different moral commitments while remaining philosophically neutral regarding the correctness of those commitments.

Separating motivational explanation from ethical evaluation also provides greater conceptual clarity by distinguishing psychological description from normative philosophy.

---

## Philosophical Connections

This question intersects with several areas of philosophy, including:

- David Hume's Is–Ought distinction
- G. E. Moore's Naturalistic Fallacy
- Nietzsche's genealogical method
- Evolutionary ethics
- Moral psychology
- Descriptive versus normative philosophy

---

## Outstanding Questions

- Can any motivational theory remain completely value-neutral?
- Does describing motivation inevitably privilege certain motivational systems?
- At what point does motivational psychology become ethics?
- Can descriptive theories ever remain entirely independent of normative assumptions?
- How should explanatory and ethical theories interact without becoming confused?

---

## Future Directions

Future work will examine whether the descriptive commitments of the IMSF can be maintained while applying the framework to moral decision-making, political philosophy, and historical case studies.

The challenge is to preserve explanatory neutrality without diminishing the framework's usefulness in understanding ethically significant behaviour.
