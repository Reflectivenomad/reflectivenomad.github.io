Primary vs. Secondary Motivational Systems
Research Question
What distinguishes a motivational system that directly prescribes behavior from a psychological factor that merely modifies behavioral prioritization?
Central Problem
The framework distinguishes primary motivational systems from secondary modifier systems, but the distinction requires more rigorous criteria. Without clear criteria, there is a risk of endlessly multiplying "primary" motivations whenever new psychological phenomena are encountered.
Preliminary Criteria
A primary motivational system may satisfy several conditions:
It independently prescribes a possible course of action.
It can compete directly with other motivational systems.
It remains intelligible even in the absence of particular emotional states.
It possesses explanatory necessity rather than explanatory convenience.
By contrast, a secondary modifier system:
Does not prescribe behavior independently.
Alters the weighting of existing motivational systems.
Requires an active motivational conflict in order to influence behavior.
Explains behavioral variability rather than behavioral direction.
Outstanding Questions
Can empirical evidence distinguish modifiers from motivations?
Could some psychological phenomena occupy an intermediate position?
Should the distinction be understood functionally rather than structurally?
