# Before Consciousness Notices

## Thinking with Global Workspace Theory

*Research notes: 2 July 2026*

There is a moment in every decision that almost escapes observation.

Not the moment we act.

Not even the moment we become consciously aware of choosing.

I mean the instant just before one possibility becomes the one we experience ourselves considering.

That moment has been bothering me for some time.

Global Workspace Theory offers one of the most influential accounts of consciousness in contemporary cognitive science. In broad terms, conscious awareness emerges when particular information gains access to a global workspace, making it available to numerous cognitive processes at once. Countless unconscious activities continue in parallel, yet only a small fraction ever enters conscious awareness.

It is an elegant picture.

The more I read it, the more one question quietly refused to disappear.

What determines what reaches the workspace in the first place?

Attention is an obvious candidate.

Memory contributes.

Perception undoubtedly plays a role.

Yet I found myself returning to something slightly different.

Suppose two behavioural possibilities are simultaneously available.

Leave.

Stay.

Speak.

Remain silent.

Help.

Protect yourself.

By the time one of those possibilities becomes the focus of conscious deliberation, has something already happened?

The question began to shift the way I was reading the theory.

Global Workspace Theory explains how information becomes globally available.

It says remarkably little about why one motivational possibility defeats another before entering consciousness.

Perhaps that silence is intentional.

Perhaps it simply belongs to another explanatory level.

I found the distinction surprisingly helpful.

For a long time I had unconsciously treated conscious deliberation as though it were the place where motivational conflict occurred.

Increasingly, that seems incomplete.

Consciousness may be less like a courtroom in which motivations argue and more like a stage upon which the outcome of an earlier competition becomes visible.

The analogy is imperfect.

Competitions undoubtedly continue within conscious reflection itself. New information appears. Priorities change. Decisions are revised. Consciousness clearly participates in motivational life.

Yet participation is not necessarily origin.

That possibility has remained with me.

It also raises another question.

Suppose motivational competition begins before conscious awareness.

What, then, is consciousness contributing?

Perhaps not competition itself.

Perhaps integration.

The motivational landscape arriving in awareness is rarely experienced as a collection of isolated impulses. Instead, it appears as a coherent situation containing memories, emotions, expectations, imagined consequences and behavioural possibilities. Conscious reflection may therefore concern itself less with generating motivations than with integrating their consequences into a unified perspective from which action becomes possible.

I am drawn to that thought.

Not because it diminishes consciousness.

Quite the opposite.

It grants consciousness a role that feels uniquely human without requiring it to generate every motivational impulse from the beginning.

Global Workspace Theory never set out to explain motivational organisation.

Nor should it be criticised for failing to answer a question it never claimed to address.

If anything, reading it has clarified the boundaries of my own curiosity.

I am becoming less interested in consciousness as the birthplace of decisions.

I am becoming more interested in the competitions that quietly prepare the possibilities consciousness eventually inherits.

Perhaps the most important events in decision-making occur before we ever become aware that a decision is taking shape.

If that is true, consciousness may illuminate motivational conflict without originating it.

For now, that possibility feels worth following.
