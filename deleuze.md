# The Self That Refuses to Stay Still

## Thinking with Gilles Deleuze

Some philosophers answer your questions.

Others quietly dismantle the assumptions hidden inside them.

Reading Deleuze often felt like the latter.

I realised this somewhere in the middle of a passage I had already read three times. I was trying to understand what he meant by *becoming*, but the harder I tried to pin the idea down, the more I suspected I was asking the wrong kind of question. Deleuze did not seem interested in describing what things *are*. He was far more interested in how they continuously change.

That shift unsettled me more than I expected.

Most discussions of motivation quietly assume a relatively stable individual making decisions. We ask why a person chose one action instead of another as though the chooser remains essentially unchanged throughout the process. Deliberation is treated as something the self performs.

Deleuze invites a more uncomfortable possibility.

**What if deliberation transforms the self doing the deliberating?**

At first, the suggestion felt almost exaggerated. Surely some continuity must remain. Otherwise memory, commitment and responsibility become difficult to explain.

Yet the more I reflected on ordinary experience, the harder it became to dismiss entirely.

How often have I finished a difficult decision feeling like a slightly different person from the one who began it?

Not dramatically different.

Just enough that the reasons which seemed decisive an hour earlier no longer carried the same force.

It is tempting to dismiss this as merely changing one's mind.

Deleuze seems to suggest something more radical.

Perhaps the mind itself is never the fixed object we imagine it to be.

For several days I tried fitting this into the language I had already become comfortable using. I looked for motivational systems, stable structures and recurring patterns.

Deleuze resisted every attempt.

His writing seemed almost suspicious of anything that appeared too permanent.

Oddly enough, that resistance became productive.

It forced me to distinguish between two different kinds of stability.

A river changes continuously, yet we still recognise it as the same river.

A conversation changes with every sentence, yet it remains recognisably the same discussion.

Perhaps continuity sometimes belongs less to fixed components than to ongoing organisation.

That thought stayed with me long after I had closed the book.

It also softened one of my own assumptions.

Perhaps asking whether the self is stable or changing is already too blunt a question.

Human beings seem capable of remarkable continuity without remaining psychologically identical from one moment to the next.

Whatever persists does so without freezing development.

I do not know whether Deleuze would be satisfied with that conclusion.

Perhaps he would argue that I am still holding on to stability more tightly than I should.

Perhaps he would be right.

What I do know is that I no longer think of deliberation as something occurring inside an untouched decision-maker.

Every meaningful decision appears to leave behind a slightly altered person.

Whether that alteration is best understood as transformation, becoming or simply learning is a question I am not yet prepared to settle.

For now, I am content with a smaller realisation.

Sometimes philosophy does not answer the question you brought to it.

It quietly teaches you why the question was too small.
