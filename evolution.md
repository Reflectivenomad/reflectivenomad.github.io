# The Question Evolution Never Claimed to Answer

## Thinking with Evolutionary Psychology

*Research notes: March 2026*

There is a peculiar habit in philosophy.

When two theories speak about the same subject, we immediately assume they must be competing.

I have found myself resisting that instinct while reading evolutionary psychology.

The resistance did not come because I doubted evolution. On the contrary, it came because I slowly realised that I had been asking evolution a question it never claimed to answer.

Why do human beings possess fear?

Evolution has an answer.

Why do we seek attachment?

Evolution has an answer.

Why does curiosity persist across cultures?

Again, evolution offers a remarkably persuasive account.

For a while, I imagined those answers were also explanations of motivation itself.

Then something began to bother me.

Suppose evolution explains why hunger exists.

It still leaves another question quietly untouched.

When hunger, loyalty, curiosity and fear all become active at the same moment, why does one prevail?

The silence surrounding that question is not a weakness.

It simply belongs to another conversation.

That distinction became clearer the more I read.

Evolution is fundamentally historical. It asks how traits emerged, persisted and spread across generations. Its explanations unfold over thousands of years. The unit of analysis is not the individual making a decision this afternoon, but populations changing across evolutionary time.

Motivational conflict unfolds on an entirely different timescale.

It happens in seconds.

Perhaps even milliseconds.

The questions are no longer historical.

They become organisational.

What surprised me was how easily those different questions become confused.

If someone explains that attachment evolved because it increased reproductive success, one almost feels as though motivation itself has been explained. Yet an evolutionary origin does not automatically reveal how attachment behaves when it competes with self-preservation, ambition, curiosity or moral conviction.

Origins and organisation are neighbours.

They are not identical.

Oddly enough, I found myself thinking less about biology and more about architecture.

Knowing why bricks were invented tells me remarkably little about how a cathedral remains standing.

The historical explanation matters.

The structural explanation matters too.

Neither replaces the other.

That image stayed with me.

It also softened an assumption I had not realised I was carrying.

I had unconsciously treated explanations as though they belonged to a single hierarchy, each competing to become the deepest account. Increasingly, that seems mistaken. Scientific explanations often coexist precisely because they answer different questions.

The chemist does not replace the biologist.

The biologist does not replace the psychologist.

Perhaps motivational organisation occupies a similar position.

If so, the relationship with evolutionary psychology becomes much less adversarial than I first imagined. One account explains why motivational systems exist at all. Another attempts to understand what happens once those systems coexist within a single individual.

Neither seems complete without the other.

Johnny once remarked that evolution is a universal acid.

The phrase lingered with me longer than I expected.

There is truth in it.

Evolution reaches astonishingly far.

Yet even the most powerful acid does not dissolve every distinction worth making.

Some questions survive.

I closed the literature on evolutionary psychology with considerably more admiration than when I began.

Not because it answered my own question.

Because it helped me finally understand what my question actually was.
