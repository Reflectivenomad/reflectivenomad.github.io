# What Makes a Motivation Primary?

## Thinking with Jaak Panksepp

...
Every framework eventually reaches a point where it must become uncomfortably precise.
Mine arrived with what initially seemed like an innocent question.
What exactly makes a motivational system primary?
The distinction appeared straightforward when I first encountered it. Hunger seemed primary. Curiosity seemed primary. Attachment, self-preservation and perhaps even meaning appeared to prescribe behaviour directly. Other phenomena—emotion, personality, social conditioning—appeared to modify how those motivations expressed themselves.
Simple enough.
Until I tried explaining why.
The difficulty became impossible to ignore while reading Jaak Panksepp. His affective neuroscience proposes a number of evolutionarily ancient emotional systems that organise behaviour across mammalian species. SEEKING, FEAR, CARE, PLAY, LUST, RAGE and PANIC are not decorative features of the mind; they are deeply embedded biological organisations.
At first glance, they seemed to fit comfortably into my own thinking.
Then something began to bother me.
Suppose fear is a primary motivation.
What, exactly, does it prescribe?
Escape?
Not always.
People sometimes run towards danger.
Sometimes they freeze.
Sometimes they fight.
Sometimes they remain behind because someone else cannot escape.
The emotion remains recognisable.
The behaviour changes.
That observation became strangely difficult to dismiss.
Perhaps the important question is not whether fear changes behaviour—it clearly does—but whether it specifies the behavioural objective itself.
The distinction is subtle.
A compass determines direction.
The wind determines how difficult the journey becomes.
Both influence movement.
Only one determines the destination.
I found myself wondering whether emotions resemble the wind more than the compass.
The analogy is imperfect, but it continues returning.
Curiosity complicates the picture further. Curiosity often appears to prescribe exploration directly. Yet even curiosity seems capable of serving remarkably different ends. One person investigates a scientific problem. Another searches for gossip. Another wanders through a forest with no destination at all.
Perhaps motivations never exist in isolation.
Perhaps they always arrive entangled with emotional tone, memory, personality and social context, making the search for perfectly isolated motivational systems more difficult than I initially imagined.
If so, the distinction between primary and secondary may not describe separate psychological objects at all.
It may instead describe different explanatory roles.
Some processes appear to answer the question,
"What behaviour is being pursued?"
Others seem better suited to answering,
"Why does this behaviour prevail here rather than another?"
That possibility feels more satisfying than my original formulation.
Not because it settles the issue.
Because it changes the question.
I began reading Panksepp expecting to classify emotions.
Instead, I left wondering whether classification itself had distracted me from something more important.
Perhaps psychology is less like arranging books onto shelves than trying to understand a conversation in which everyone keeps speaking at once.
I have not yet decided whether emotions belong among primary motivations.
What I know with greater confidence is that asking why one behavioural possibility defeats another has become a far more interesting problem than deciding which psychological category emotion should occupy.
