# Freud Refuses to Leave

...

There are philosophers one reads because history insists upon it. Freud is different. Even after people announce that they have abandoned him, he continues quietly inhabiting their vocabulary. We still speak of repression, unconscious motives, projection, rationalisation, denial, and internal conflict with remarkable ease. It is difficult to decide whether this is because Freud understood the mind unusually well or because he permanently altered the way we describe ourselves.

That uncertainty was enough to make me return to him.

What struck me almost immediately was that Freud seems profoundly suspicious of appearances. A person's explanation of their own behaviour is rarely accepted at face value. Behind every conscious justification lies the possibility of another story—one that the individual either cannot see or perhaps cannot bear to see. It is a remarkably unsettling way of approaching human psychology. The self ceases to be the final authority on itself.

I found myself resisting this.

Not because unconscious influences seem implausible. Everyday life offers too many examples of people surprising themselves to dismiss the unconscious entirely. Rather, I wondered whether Freud sometimes asks too much of hidden motives. If every explanation conceals another explanation, where does the search finally end? At what point does psychology stop uncovering and begin inventing?

The question lingered.

Freud's answer to internal conflict is equally ambitious. The id desires, the superego judges, and the ego negotiates between demands that rarely point in the same direction. It is an elegant architecture. More importantly, it takes conflict seriously. Human beings are not inconsistent because they occasionally fail to think clearly; they are inconsistent because different aspects of the psyche genuinely pull in different directions.

I admire that intuition.

Yet somewhere during my reading, my attention drifted away from the architecture itself.

I became less interested in the ego than in the negotiation it performs.

Suppose two incompatible behavioural possibilities are competing for expression. Does that necessarily require separate psychological agencies? Or could the conflict emerge from the interaction of competing motivational organisations without introducing distinct structures of mind? The negotiation seems undeniable. The negotiator feels less obvious.

Perhaps this is simply another way of describing the same phenomenon.

Or perhaps it is a fundamentally different question.

Freud appears to ask who is struggling inside us. Increasingly, I find myself asking what is struggling.

The distinction is subtle enough that I almost overlooked it.

It also changes the direction of inquiry.

Once the focus shifts from psychological agencies to behavioural prescriptions, conflict begins to look less like a courtroom and more like a landscape. Different concerns become prominent under different conditions. Some fade almost entirely before returning unexpectedly years later. The mind begins to resemble an organisation whose balance is continually changing rather than a tribunal permanently occupied by the same three officials.

I am not convinced Freud would recognise this picture.

Then again, I am not convinced he would entirely reject it either.

Perhaps the greatest mistake one can make with Freud is treating his language too literally. The id, ego and superego may matter less as anatomical discoveries than as an insistence that human beings are rarely as unified as they imagine themselves to be. On that point, I find it surprisingly difficult to disagree with him.

I finished Freud with an unexpected feeling.

Not that I had escaped his influence.

Only that I had begun asking a different question from the one he thought needed answering.
