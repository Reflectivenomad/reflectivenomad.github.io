# When Description Becomes Prescription

## Thinking with David Hume

*Research notes: 20–27 June 2026*

It has become almost impossible for me to discuss motivation without someone eventually asking whether a particular motivation is good, bad, rational or irrational.

The transition happens so naturally that I often fail to notice it until the conversation has already changed.

We begin by asking why people behave as they do.

Somewhere along the way, without anyone announcing it, the discussion becomes one about how people ought to behave instead.

The shift fascinates me.

It also makes me uneasy.

A theory that begins by describing behaviour can quietly become a theory that judges behaviour, often without intending to. That possibility had been lingering in the background of my own work long before I returned to Hume. Reading him simply forced me to confront it directly.

His famous observation—that one cannot derive an ought from an is—is usually introduced in ethics lectures as though it were primarily a moral principle.

I suspect I had underestimated it.

The more I thought about it, the less it seemed like a statement about morality and the more it appeared to be a warning about explanation itself.

Description and evaluation are neighbours.

They are not the same house.

The distinction sounds almost trivial until one tries to maintain it.

Suppose someone risks their own life to save a stranger. A descriptive account may explain the interaction of attachment, empathy, identity, duty, self-preservation and countless other influences. Nothing in that explanation, however detailed, tells us whether the action was morally admirable.

Likewise, explaining an act of cruelty does not excuse it, nor does explaining generosity celebrate it.

The explanation remains silent.

At first, that silence felt unsatisfying.

Surely understanding human motivation should eventually tell us something about how we ought to live.

The temptation is remarkably strong.

We admire certain motivations.

We distrust others.

Before long, our descriptions begin leaning toward our preferences.

Hume keeps pulling them back.

That restraint has gradually become more important to me than I expected.

The framework I have been developing is frequently interpreted as though it intends to recommend which motivations deserve to prevail. Yet that has never quite been the question occupying me.

My curiosity has always been directed elsewhere.

Why does one behavioural possibility triumph over another?

How do competing motivations become organised into coherent action?

Those questions remain descriptive even when the behaviours themselves carry profound moral significance.

Perhaps this is where Hume has quietly altered my thinking.

A descriptive theory does not become incomplete simply because it refuses to issue prescriptions.

Anatomy does not tell us how bodies ought to move.

Astronomy does not tell planets where they ought to orbit.

Their task is explanation, not instruction.

Perhaps motivation deserves the same intellectual discipline.

That realisation brought an unexpected consequence.

If motivational theory remains descriptive, then disagreements about morality need not become disagreements about motivational architecture.

Two individuals may condemn or celebrate the same action while agreeing entirely about the processes that produced it.

Conversely, they may share identical moral commitments while disagreeing profoundly about how motivation itself operates.

I had not appreciated that separation before reading Hume.

Oddly enough, it makes the descriptive project feel more ambitious rather than less.

It asks us to understand human behaviour without immediately trying to improve it.

That is far more difficult than it sounds.

I did not leave Hume with a new theory of motivation.

I left with something quieter.

An explanation should earn the right to remain an explanation.

The moment it begins telling people how they ought to live, it has crossed into a different conversation.

There is nothing wrong with that conversation.

It simply is not the one I am trying to have.
